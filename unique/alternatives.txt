Restricted strictly to steady-state geometry non-uniqueness, isothermal/temperate setting:

- Schoof (2007) grounding-line hysteresis is a genuine steady-state-geometry
  multiplicity result: for fixed external forcing (sea level, accumulation),
  an overdeepened bed admits more than one steady grounding-line
  position/profile. So this is a real peer to the flat-fill result, just via
  flotation/bed-slope rather than via the flat-fill degeneracy. The land-only,
  a=0 case is untouched by it.

- Non-monotone sliding laws are set aside. That mechanism is about
  non-uniqueness of velocity for a given fixed domain, not about multiple
  steady geometries. It doesn't itself produce a second steady-state surface
  unless that velocity bistability is then shown to feed back into the
  continuity equation and pin down two different steady s. Nobody in the
  literature surveyed does that. So it's not actually a competing steady-state
  mechanism, just an asterisk on the Jouvet-Rappaz result (uniqueness of
  velocity/pressure for known geometry) -- it should not be placed alongside
  Schoof as if it were the same kind of claim.

So, the only other mechanism in the literature for steady-state geometry
non-uniqueness in the isothermal/temperate setting is Schoof/Weertman's
overdeepened-bed grounding-line hysteresis (marine, flotation-driven).
Elevation-accumulation feedback (Bodvarsson/Weertman 1961, Jouvet et al. 2011
non-existence) is the other known family. The flat-fill construction is
mechanistically distinct from both -- no flotation, no elevation feedback,
just geometric degeneracy of the obstacle problem at bedrock minima under
a identically 0.


Jouvet & Rappaz (2011) exact assumptions on the sliding boundary condition
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Domain split: boundary of Omega is Gamma_N (free surface) union Gamma_R
(sliding) union Gamma_D (no-slip), with Gamma_N, Gamma_R assumed C^1 and
Gamma_D nonempty.

The sliding condition itself, on Gamma_R (their eq. 2.4-2.5):

    u . n = 0,
    2 mu(eps(u)) eps(u) . n . t_i = - alpha(|u|) u . t_i,

i.e. no-penetration plus a nonlinear Robin (friction-type) law tangentially,
with sliding coefficient

    alpha(t) = c (t + t_0)^(1/n - 1),

t = |u|, c > 0, n >= 1 Glen's exponent, and t_0 > 0 a regularization
preventing alpha -> infinity as t -> 0. No-slip on Gamma_D is u = 0
(Dirichlet), recovered formally as Gamma_R = empty set.

Key structural assumption for uniqueness: the proof goes through a
potential/minimization functional J with friction term N(x) = integral_0^x
t alpha(t) dt added to the viscous term M(x) = integral_0^x s mu(s) ds.
Uniqueness (their Theorem 3.8/3.10) rests on strict convexity of J, which
in turn relies on alpha's specific power-law monotonicity (same Glen
exponent structure as mu, via the regularized form
alpha(t) = c(t+t_0)^(1/n-1)) -- i.e. alpha is not an arbitrary sliding law;
it is tied to the same n and regularized the same way as the viscosity,
which is what makes N strictly convex.

So: their uniqueness theorem does cover sliding, not just no-slip -- but
only for this specific regularized power-law sliding coefficient. A
general/non-monotone sliding law (e.g. Schoof's Coulomb-type law, which
they cite as an alternative in the introduction but do not analyze) is
outside their hypotheses. This is the precise caveat for the "asterisk on
Jouvet-Rappaz" point above: their fixed-geometry velocity uniqueness holds
for this regularized nonlinear-Robin sliding law (and no-slip as the
Gamma_R = empty set special case), not for arbitrary/non-monotone friction
laws.


Jouvet, Rappaz, Bueler, Blatter (2011) exact uniqueness claim
---------------------------------------------------------------

This is Jouvetetal2011 in the bib: J. Glaciol. 57(202):345-354,
"Existence and stability of steady-state solutions of the shallow-ice-sheet
equation by an energy-minimization approach." Ed Bueler is a co-author.

On uniqueness, verbatim/paraphrased:
- Theorem 1 proves existence of a minimizer of the energy functional J
  on a flat bed, fixed margin, non-sliding, with mass balance
  b(z) = a_m(z - z_ELA) linear in elevation (their case 1). Immediately
  after: "our understanding of the calculus of variations ... fails to
  resolve the uniqueness of eta because the function
  eta -> -(alpha/q) eta^q + alpha eta is not convex." Uniqueness is
  explicitly left open there, not proven, not claimed -- just an
  acknowledged obstruction from lack of convexity.
- Conclusions section reiterates: "Due to a lack of convexity, uniqueness
  of the solution needs to be investigated by a different approach."
- Numerically: "uniqueness of the steady solution is not proven
  rigorously, [but] all steady solutions found by numerical iteration
  yielded the same result, independent of the initial geometry" -- i.e.
  empirical/numerical support only, for their fixed-margin, flat-bed,
  elevation-dependent-SMB case.

Bed/scope, consistent with the rest of this file: flat bed throughout,
fixed margin (not a free-boundary obstacle problem) for the existence
theorem. The free-margin version of the same (elevation-dependent) problem
is proven to have NO solution -- the non-existence result already cited in
the paper draft.

Directly relevant: in the conclusions they flag almost exactly the open
question the flat-fill construction now answers: "Further research is
needed to resolve basic existence questions with sliding and/or non-flat
bed, even in simpler cases where mass balance is independent of surface
elevation." That is essentially naming the open problem -- general bed,
elevation-independent SMB -- which the flat-fill construction (Proposition
notunique) now answers in the negative (non-uniqueness, via local minima
of a non-flat bed under a=0). Ed Bueler flagged this gap himself in 2011
and is now closing it.

Net: no rigorous uniqueness claim anywhere in Jouvetetal2011, for any
case -- only a noted convexity obstruction and numerical-only support,
plus an explicit acknowledgment that the general-bed / elevation-
independent case was open.
