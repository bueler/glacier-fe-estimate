Restricted strictly to steady-state geometry non-uniqueness, isothermal/temperate setting:

- Schoof (2007) grounding-line hysteresis is a genuine steady-state-geometry
  multiplicity result: for fixed external forcing (sea level, accumulation),
  an overdeepened bed admits more than one steady grounding-line
  position/profile. So this is a real peer to the flat-fill result, just via
  flotation/bed-slope rather than via the flat-fill degeneracy. The land-only,
  a=0 case is untouched by it.

- Non-monotone sliding laws are set aside. That mechanism is about
  non-uniqueness of velocity for a given fixed domain, not about multiple
  steady geometries. It doesn't itself produce a second steady-state surface
  unless that velocity bistability is then shown to feed back into the
  continuity equation and pin down two different steady s. Nobody in the
  literature surveyed does that. So it's not actually a competing steady-state
  mechanism, just an asterisk on the Jouvet-Rappaz hypothesis (uniqueness of
  velocity/pressure for known geometry) -- it should not be placed alongside
  Schoof as if it were the same kind of claim.

So, the only other mechanism in the literature for steady-state geometry
non-uniqueness in the isothermal/temperate setting is Schoof/Weertman's
overdeepened-bed grounding-line hysteresis (marine, flotation-driven).
Elevation-accumulation feedback (Bodvarsson/Weertman 1961, Jouvet et al. 2011
non-existence) is the other known family. The flat-fill construction is
mechanistically distinct from both -- no flotation, no elevation feedback,
just geometric degeneracy of the obstacle problem at bedrock minima under
a identically 0.
