\abstract{We consider the form of Reynold's transport theorem for time-dependent 3D sets of the form
    $$\Lam(t) = \{(x,y,z) : b(x,y) < z < s(t,x,y)\} \subset \RR^3$$
that is, for a changing glacier set defined by a time-independent lower surface $z=b(x,y)$ and a time-dependent upper surface $z=s(t,x,y)$.  We are assuming that the boundary of $\Lam(t)$, up to a curve in 3D which is a set of measure zero, is made up only of a lower and an upper surface.  (This neglects any cliffs along the margin of the glacier.)

\subsection*{Definitions for a moving glacier surface}  Consider a fixed map-plane domain $\Omega \subset \RR^2$.  All calculations to follow are defined within this set, or on proper subsets of it, or on three-dimensional sets defined over it.

Suppose that the bed elevation of the glacier is given by a continuous function $b(x,y)$, so it is time-independent.  This same function is the surface elevation in unglaciated areas.

We will allow the upper surface to change in time.  However, we will define it in a manner which is compatible with digital elevation models, that is, so that there is a single surface elevation which is the glacier surface on ice-covered land but which is the bed elevation otherwise:
	$$s(t,x,y) = \begin{cases} s(t,x,y), & \text{if } s(t,x,y)>b(x,y) \text{ (glacier-covered)} \\
	                           b(x,y), & \text{otherwise (ice-free)} \end{cases}$$
Now both $b(x,y)$ and $s(t,x,y)$ are defined for any $(x,y)\in \Omega$, and for every $t$ in some duration $[0,T]$ of interest.

Let
    $$D(t) = \{(x,y) : s(t,x,y) > b(x,y)\} \subset \Omega \subset \RR^2.$$
This map-plane set is the horizontal footprint of the glacier, the time-dependent ice-covered area where the layer has positive thickness $h = s-b > 0$.  In alternative notation we might write $D(t) = \Lambda(t)^\top$, that is, $D(t)$ is the (open) vertical projection of $\Lambda(t)$.

Finally, let $\gamma(t) = \partial D(t)$.  This is the glacier margin curve along which the thickness first vanishes as one comes off the glacier.  By continuity, $s=b$ at points of $\gamma(t)$.  Generally $\gamma(t)$ has measure zero at every time, so that $\int_{\gamma(t)} f\,dx\,dy = 0$ for any function $f$, but it turns out that we will not need this fact, though we do need the fact that $\gamma(t)$ is measurable, which follows from continuity of $b$ and $s$.

We may write the surface using a level set function,
	$$f(t,x,y,z) = z - s(t,x,y),$$
so that the upper surface is $f=0$.  Then $\nabla f = (-s_x,\,-s_y,\,1)$ and $|\nabla f| = \sqrt{1+s_x^2+s_y^2}$.  It follows that, because the gradient of $f$ is orthogonal to its level surfaces, the upward unit normal on the surface is $\hat n = \grad f/|\grad f|$ or
    $$\hat n = \frac{(-s_x,\,-s_y,\,1)}{\sqrt{1+s_x^2+s_y^2}}.$$

We next consider the (scalar) velocity of the surface in the direction normal to the surface.  To derive this, note that a path $\vec x(t)$ tracking the surface, i.e.~of a non-material (conceptual) particle that is always on the surface, satisfies $f(t,\vec x(t)) = 0$ and thus $f_t + \nabla f \cdot \vec x' = 0$ by the chain rule.  We may split $\vec x' = u_n \hat n + \vec v_\parallel$ with $\vec v_\parallel$ tangential.  However, since $\nabla f$ is normal to the surface, $\nabla f\cdot \vec v_\parallel = 0$, and since $\hat n$ is parallel to $\grad f$ so $\grad f \cdot \hat n = |\grad f|$, this leaves $f_t + |\nabla f|\,u_n = 0$.  It follows that $u_n = -f_t/|\nabla f|$, so by differentiating the level set function $f$ from its definition we have
    $$u_n = \frac{s_t}{\sqrt{1+s_x^2+s_y^2}}$$
as the normal velocity of the surface.  The tangential part of the (vector) velocity of the surface is an arbitrary parametrization choice, so it does not contribute to $u_n$, and thus we need not define it carefully.

\subsection*{Reynold's transport theorem for glacier geometry}

Now let $\rho(t,x,y,z)$ be the (variable) glacier density in mass-per-volume units.  Suppose
	$$\pi(t,x,y,z) = \rho(t,x,y,z) \phi(t,x,y,z)$$
is the physical quantity is being transported, an abstract density.  The abstract function $\phi$ might have no units (e.g.~we will use $\phi=1$ below in a first example), or units of energy-per-mass like (specific) enthalpy, so that $\pi$ is an energy density (energy-per-volume), and so on.  Note that $\phi$, thus $\pi$, can be a scalar, vector, or tensor.

Define
    $$F(t) = \int_{\Lam(t)} \pi(t,x,y,z) \, dx\,dy\,dz,$$
or $F=\int_{\Lam} \pi \, dV$ for short.  This is the total amount of the quantity $\pi$ in the time-dependent glacier domain $\Lambda(t)$.  Our goal is to understand and compute the derivative $F'(t)$.

By Fubini's theorem, $F = \int_{D} \left[\int_{b}^{s} \pi \, dz\right] dx\,dy$.  More precisely, if we let
    $$g(t,x,y) = \int_{b(x,y)}^{s(t,x,y)} \pi(t,x,y,z) \, dz$$
then we may write
	$$F(t) = \int_{D(t)} g(t,x,y) dx\,dy,$$
from Fubini's theorem, or $F=\int_{\Lam} g \, dA$ for short.  Note that $g$ can only be nonzero at $x,y \in D(t)$, that is, it can only be nonzero in ice-filled columns where $s$ exceeds $b$; we will return to this idea below.

To find the ordinary derivative of $F$ we need to differentiate $g$ with respect to $t$ (partial derivative).  Since the $b_t=0$, only the upper limit contributes a boundary term when we calculate the derivative by the familiar Leibniz rule:
    $$g_t(t,x,y) = \int_{b(x,y)}^{s(t,x,y)} \pi_t(t,x,y,z)\,dz + \pi(t,x,y,s(t,x,y))\, s_t(t,x,y).$$
This formula applies everywhere on $\Omega$, but note that at points $(x,y)$ outside of $D(t)$ we have $\int_b^s f \,dz=0$ (any $f$) and $s_t=0$.  In fact, $g$ and $g_t$ are zero outside of $D(t)$.

However, the map-plane ice-covered domain $D(t) \subset \Omega$ changes with time, and it has a moving boundary $\gamma(t)$.  Now, the 2D form of Reynold's transport theorem, applied over $D(t)\subset \Omega$, gives
    $$\frac{d}{dt}\left(\int_{D(t)} g(t,x,y)\,dx\,dy\right) = \int_{D(t)} g_t(t,x,y)\,dx\,dy \;+\; \int_{\gamma(t)} g\, u_\gamma \, d\ell,$$
where $u_\gamma$ is the outward normal velocity of the margin curve $\gamma(t)$ within the $(x,y)$-plane, i.e.~within $\Omega$.  It is a scalar function along this curve.\footnote{The scalar velocity $u_\gamma$---or ``speed'' but it can be of either sign---is well-defined even though the full vector velocity of $\gamma$ is not meaningful.  The latter would require a parameterization choice.}

The line integral over $\gamma(t)$ in the above equation is important to consider, but in fact it vanishes\footnote{This is the same mechanism as the following 1D fact: if $F(t)=\int_{a(t)}^{b(t)} g(t,x)\,dx$, with $g$ vanishing at both endpoints for all $t$, namely $g(t,a(t))=g(t,b(t))=0$, then $F'(t) = \int_{a(t)}^{b(t)} g_t(t,x)\,dx$ with no boundary-motion terms, even though $a(t),b(t)$ move.  The vanishing of the integrand at the moving boundary kills the boundary-motion contribution.} because the value of $g$ along $\gamma(t)$ is zero.  That is, $g(t,x,y)\big|_{\gamma(t)} = \int_b^b \pi\,dz = 0$.  The measure of $\gamma(t)$ is thus not important here, though the explicit hypothesis of admissibility, namely $s\ge b$, is needed, as is the continuity of $s$ and $b$.  Also, $\pi$ must stay bounded as we approach $\gamma(t)$, so that the vanishing integral argument is not defeated by an integrand which blows up competitively.  A sufficient integrability assumption on $\pi$ need not be as strong as assuming continuity.

At this point we have a useful result.  It is a form of Reynold's theorem well-suited to continuum discussions of glaciers.  We see that despite the fact that $D(t)$ changes in time, and despite the motion of the glacier margin, the form of Reynold's transport theorem is actually the same as what holds for a glacier constrained by walls and subject to $s>b$ everywhere in $D(t)$.

\begin{lem*}  With the above hypotheses,
\begin{align*}
\frac{d}{dt}\left(\int_{\Lam(t)} \pi(t,x,y,z)\,dx\,dy\,dz\right) &= \int_{\Lam(t)} \pi_t(t,x,y,z)\,dx\,dy\,dz \\
   &\qquad + \int_{D(t)} \pi(t,x,y,s(t,x,y))\, s_t(t,x,y)\,dx\,dy.
\end{align*}
With simpler notation, suppressing dependencies and the definition of $F$, we write
    $$F'(t) = \int_{\Lam} \pi_t\,dV
+ \int_{D} \pi|_s \, s_t\,dA.$$
\end{lem*}

Writing the last term as a true surface integral over the graph
$S_{top}(t)$ of $s$: recall $u_n^{top} = s_t/\sqrt{1+s_x^2+s_y^2}$ and the
area element satisfies $dA_{top} = \sqrt{1+s_x^2+s_y^2}\,dx\,dy$, so
\[
\pi\, s_t\, dx\,dy = \pi\, u_n^{top}\, dA_{top},
\]
recovering the standard form
\[
\frac{d}{dt}\int_{\Lam(t)}\pi\,dV = \int_{\Lam(t)}\partial_t\pi\,dV
+ \int_{S_{top}(t)} \pi\, u_n\, dA
+ \underbrace{\int_{S_{bot}}\pi\cdot 0\,dA}_{=0}
+ \underbrace{(\text{margin wall})}_{=0}.
\]
This matches the picture that $\partial\Lam(t)$ is top $\cup$ bottom
$\cup$ (measure-zero curve): the bottom term vanishes because the bed is
fixed ($u_n=0$), and the lateral/margin term vanishes as shown above,
leaving only the top-surface flux.

This is exactly the structure behind vertically-integrated conservation
laws (e.g.\ thickness/mass-conservation equations for a layer with a
wet/dry or grounding-line-type margin) --- one gets to treat the footprint
as if it were not moving, for free, as long as the transported quantity is
admissible (finite) at zero thickness.
