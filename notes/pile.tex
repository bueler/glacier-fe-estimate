\begin{abstract}
This is a very preliminary note, and really a dumping ground.  I have no idea where it is going.

It considers how to solve the main complementarity problem for the surface elevation of a glacier, in the notional case where the actual surface value of the ice-flow velocity is replaced by a fixed velocity field.  Under such a severe simplification, the snow accumulates or ablates at locations which then move along pre-determined characteristic curves (thus the title).  The piles of snow, assumed to have the density of ice, act like queues with a positive length, that is, the glacier thickness is positive.  A Skorohod-type formula for the solutions along characteristics solves the problem.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Consider snow which falls at some rate $a(t,x,y)$, over a time interval $t\in [0,T)$ and for locations $(x,y)$ within a fixed map-plane domain $\Omega \subset \RR^2$.  More precisely, suppose $a$ gives the \emph{signed} instantaneous surface mass balance rate \cite{Cogleyetal2011} in ice-equivalent meters per second.  This means that $a>0$ when snow is falling ($a=$ accumulation rate) and $a<0$ when melt is occurring ($a=$ ablation rate).

While in actuality there is a time scale for the conversion of snow into maximal-density ice, in this note we suppose the process is instantaneous, so falling snow immediately becomes flowing ice in a glacier.  Similarly, the time scale at which melt water runs off-glacier is sufficiently short that we also regard it as instantaneous.

On the other hand, suppose that the land is at elevation $b(x,y)$, which is assumed time-independent.  (The extension to $b(t,x,y)$ is actually straightforward.)  The value of $b$ is the bedrock elevation underneath the glacier, or it is the land surface elevation if the land is not ice-covered.

As snow piles-up, according to values $a>0$, it will form a glacier, which flows under the action of gravity, according to the shape of the land (e.g.~mountains) given by $b$.  However, as it flows into regions with melting conditions, where $a<0$, then it will decrease in thickness to zero.

To make this precise, let $s(t,x,y)$ be the upper surface elevation of the evolving glacier.  A \emph{surface kinematical equation} \cite{GreveBlatter2009} for the glacier applies at the surface,
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial s}{\partial t} - \bu|_s \cdot \bn_s - a = 0,  \label{eq:ske}
\end{equation}
but this is only true at points \emph{on the ice}.  Over the whole domain $\Omega$, including both ice-covered and ice-free locations, the surface can never go below the bed $b$.  Thus the mathematical model for the evolution of $s$ is, in fact, the following complementarity problem \cite{Calvoetal2003,JouvetBueler2012}:
\begin{equation}
s - b \ge 0, \quad
\frac{\partial s}{\partial t} - \bu|_s \cdot \bn_s - a \ge 0, \quad
(s - b) \left(\frac{\partial s}{\partial t} - \bu|_s \cdot \bn_s - a\right) = 0  \label{eq:ncp}
\end{equation}
In the above equations $\bu|_s$ denotes the (upper) surface value of the ice flow velocity, and
\begin{equation}
\bn_s = \left<-\frac{\partial s}{\partial x}, -\frac{\partial s}{\partial y}, 1\right> \label{eq:normal}
\end{equation}
is an upward surface-normal vector.  Equation \eqref{eq:ske} is understood by glaciologists as a way to determine the vertical motion of the surface from the surface-normal component of the ice flow velocity and the surface mass balance $a$.  Precisely-speaking equation \eqref{eq:ske} must be replaced by the complementarity problem \eqref{eq:ncp}, giving a free boundary where $s \searrow b$.  In observational practice, this free boundary is simply the location where a glaciologist will lose interest in the topic as they walk off the glacier.

Note that $h=s-b$ is the ice thickness.  Clearly, complementarity model \eqref{eq:ncp} can be re-phrased in terms of thickness, constrained by $h\ge 0$.  Indeed, by the incompressibility of the ice, the corresponding complementarity problem has divergence form when stated in terms of the thickness \cite{Bueler2021,JouvetBueler2012}.  However, the surface elevation $s$ is generally smoother than the thickness $h$, because the latter inherits the low regularity of the bed elevation data $b$ \cite{Bueler2025}, and so in this note we use $s$ to parameterize glacier geometry.

However, in a flowing glacier a 3D ice velocity field $\bu$ solves a Stokes-type problem within the ice, below the surface, which is a very-viscous fluid \cite{GreveBlatter2009}.  Thus a complete glacier evolution model must couple \eqref{eq:ncp} with equations determining the 3D velocity and pressure fields through a Stokes solution at every instant.  For such a model see equations (1.1)--(1.6) in \cite{Bueler2025}; complementarity problem \eqref{eq:ncp} is (1.3) therein.

In this note we are considering the severely-simplified model which arises if the coupled-solution velocity is replaced by a pre-determined velocity field $\bu(t,x,y) \in \RR^3$ which is defined for every $t\in [0,\infty)$ and $(x,y)\in\Omega$.  Now the velocity field $\bu$ in the surface kinematical complementarity problem \eqref{eq:ncp} is given as data:
\begin{equation}
s - b \ge 0, \quad
\frac{\partial s}{\partial t} - \bu \cdot \bn_s - a \ge 0, \quad
(s - b) \left(\frac{\partial s}{\partial t} - \bu \cdot \bn_s - a\right) = 0  \label{eq:pilencpearly}
\end{equation}
We will also suppose $s=b$ along $\partial \Omega$, that is, the glacier's thickness $h=s-b$ is forced to go to zero at the boundary of the computational domain.  In summary, $\Omega,a(t,x,y),b(x,y),\bu(t,x,y)$ are data for complementarity problem \eqref{eq:pilencpearly}, and $s(t,x,y)$ is the solution.

The interior condition \cite{KinderlehrerStampacchia1980} of complementarity problem \eqref{eq:pilencpearly} is a linear advection PDE, that is, equation \eqref{eq:ske} is now a linear advection.  Denote $\bu=\left<u,v,w\right>$ for the velocity components, and from now on denote partial derivatives with subscripts.  From \eqref{eq:normal}, the interior PDE, which applies at ice-covered locations where $s>b$, is
\begin{equation}
s_t + u s_x + v s_y = w + a \label{eq:pilepde}
\end{equation}
Here $u,v,w,a$ are scalar functions given as data, and complementarity problem \eqref{eq:pilencpearly} is written
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:pilencp}
\begin{align}
s - b &\ge 0 \\
s_t + u s_x + v s_y - w - a &\ge 0 \\
(s - b) \left(s_t + u s_x + v s_y - w - a\right) &= 0.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}


\section{Solution of the strong form by characteristics}

The linear advection PDE \eqref{eq:pilepde} can be solved by characteristics \cite[section 3.2]{Evans2010}, a classical construction which we now recall.  Here we do not need spatial boundary values for $s$.

\begin{lemma} \label{lem:chars}  Suppose $\Omega \subset \RR^2$ is a bounded domain.  Suppose $u,v,w,a$ are in $C^0([0,T) \times \Omega)$, with $u,v$ Lipschitz in $(x,y)\in\Omega$.  Let $s_0 \in C^0(\Omega)$.  Suppose $s(t,x,y)$ is in $C^0([0,T)\times \Omega) \cap C^1((0,T)\times \Omega)$ and solves \eqref{eq:pilepde} in the classical (and unconstrained) sense, with $s(0,x,y)=s_0(x,y)$ for all $(x,y)\in \Omega$.  For each $(x_0,y_0) \in \Omega$, let
    $$t \mapsto (X(t), Y(t)) = (X(t;x_0,y_0), Y(t;x_0,y_0)),$$
denote the unique continuous characteristic curve solving the ODE system
\begin{equation}
\frac{dX}{dt} = u(t,X,Y), \qquad \frac{dY}{dt} = v(t,X,Y), \label{eq:chars}
\end{equation}
with $X(0) = x_0$ and $Y(0) = y_0$.  Let $t_s = \inf \{t\in [0,T)\,:\, (X(t),Y(t))\notin\Omega\}$, with the convention that $\inf\emptyset=T$.  Then
\begin{equation}
s\!\left(t, X(t), Y(t)\right) = s_0(x_0,y_0)
  + \int_0^t \big[w\!\left(\tau, X(\tau), Y(\tau)\right) + a\!\left(\tau, X(\tau), Y(\tau)\right)\big] d\tau \label{eq:charsol}
\end{equation}
for $t\in[0,t_s)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For $(x_0,y_0)\in \Omega$, let $\tilde S(t)$ be defined by \eqref{eq:charsol}, so that $\tilde S\in C^1([0,t_s))$, $\tilde S(0)=s_0(x_0,y_0)$, and $\tilde S'(t)=e(t,X(t),Y(t))$, where $e=w+a$.  On the other hand, let $S(t)=s\!\left(t, X(t), Y(t)\right)$; note $S(0)=s_0(x_0,y_0)$.  Differentiate $S(t)$ using the chain rule, then apply \eqref{eq:chars}, and then apply \eqref{eq:pilepde}, to see that $S'(t)=e(t,X(t),Y(t))$.  Thus $\tilde S$ and $S$ are equal by the fundamental theorem of calculus.
\end{proof}

Formula \eqref{eq:charsol} becomes an explicit formula for $s(t,x,y)$ when the characteristics can be inverted.  That is, we get a formula for $s(t,x,y)$ solving the (unconstrained) PDE \eqref{eq:pilepde} if $x=X(t;x_0,y_0)$ and $y=Y(t;x_0,y_0)$ can be solved for $x_0,y_0$ in terms of $t,x,y$.  In that case we may rewrite \eqref{eq:charsol} as
\begin{equation}
s(t,x,y) = s_0(x_0,y_0)
  + \int_0^t e\!\left(\tau, X(\tau;x_0,y_0), Y(\tau;x_0,y_0)\right) d\tau \label{eq:charsolagain}
\end{equation}
where $e=w+a$, with it understood that $x_0=x_0(t,x,y)$ and $y_0=y_0(t,x,y)$.  However, some boundary value must supply the value for $s(t_s,X(t_s),Y(t_s))$ at the stopping time for that characteristic.  For simplicity we will assume, from now on, that the bed elevation is continuous up to the boundary, $b \in C^0(\bar\Omega)$, and that the ice thickness is zero at the boundary:
\begin{equation}
s(t,x,y) = b(x,y) \qquad \text{if } (x,y)\in\partial\Omega.  \label{eq:charbc}
\end{equation}
Under all these conditions we may regard the unconstrained boundary value problem \eqref{eq:pilepde}, \eqref{eq:charbc} as being solved by integration.

The main idea of this note is that we can modify the above unconstrained solution to account for the inequalities in complementarity problem \eqref{eq:pilencp}.  Along each characteristic we propose to regard this as a deterministic Skorohod problem, which arises for reflecting stochastic processes \cite{LionsSznitman1984} and in queuing theory \cite[section 1.4.1]{Stewart2011}.  The proof of the following applies \cite[Lemma 6.14]{KaratzasShreve1991}.  Note that we do \emph{not} require $b$ to be more regular than continuous.

\begin{lemma} \label{lem:skoro}  Suppose the conditions of Lemma \ref{lem:chars} hold, additionally $b,s_0 \in C^0(\bar\Omega)$, and suppose $s_0 \ge b$ on $\bar\Omega$ with $s_0=b$ on $\partial \Omega$.  For $(x_0,y_0) \in \Omega$, let $X(t),Y(t)$ be the solution of \eqref{eq:chars}, for $t\in[0,t_s)$ with $t_s=t_s(x_0,y_0)$ defined to be the first time when the characteristic curve hits $\partial\Omega$.  Define
\begin{equation}
B(t) = b(X(t),Y(t)) \quad \text{and} \quad E(t)=w(X(t),Y(t)) + a(X(t),Y(t)), \label{eq:skorodefBE}
\end{equation}
so that $B,E\in C^0([0,t_s))$.  Let $\sigma(t) = \sigma(t;x_0,y_0)$ denote the (unconstrained) solution \eqref{eq:charsol} of \eqref{eq:pilepde}, so that $\sigma \in C^0([0,t_s)) \cap C^1((0,t_s))$ and $\sigma(0)=s_0(x_0,y_0) \ge b(x_0,y_0) = B(0)$.  Define
\begin{equation}
k(t) = \max\!\left(0,\; \sup_{0 \le r \le t}\!\Big[B(r) - \sigma(r)\Big]\right),  \label{eq:skoro}
\end{equation}
so that $k$ is continuous, non-decreasing, and $k(0) = 0$.  In particular, $k$ is of bounded variation, thus differentiable a.e.~even when $B$ is not differentiable.  Then
\begin{equation}
S(t) = \sigma(t) + k(t)
\end{equation}
is continuous on $[0,t_s)$ and satisfies the complementarity problem
\begin{equation}
S - B \ge 0, \qquad S' - E \ge 0, \qquad \big(S - B\big)\,\big(S' - E\big) = 0  \label{eq:skoroncp}
\end{equation}
a.e.~$t\in [0,t_s)$.  In fact, $\sigma'=E$ for all $t$, thus $k'=S'-E$ a.e., and complementarity holds in the sense of the Stieltjes integral
\begin{equation}
\int_0^{t_s} \one_{\{t\,:\,S(t) > B(t)\}}(\tau) \,dk(\tau) = 0.  \label{eq:skorostieltjes}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

% looking at Lemma 6.14 in KaratzasShreve1991:  y(t) is H(t)=\sigma(t)-B(t) here, k(t) is k(t) here, z=0, and x(t) is S(t) here; note: y(t)+k(t) = H(t)+k(t) = \sigma(t)-B(t)+k(t) = S(t)-B(t)

\begin{proof}
Apply the Skorohod reflection formula \cite[Lemma 6.14]{KaratzasShreve1991} to $H(t) = \sigma(t) - B(t)$, the unconstrained ice thickness along the characteristic, which is allowed to go negative.  That formula yields a unique function $k$ with $H + k \ge 0$, $k$ non-decreasing, $k(0)=0$, and $(H+k)\,dk = (S-B)\,dk = 0$.  Setting $S = H + k + B = \sigma + k$ and using $\sigma' = E$ from \eqref{eq:charsol} gives \eqref{eq:skoroncp}.  Note that $\{t\,:\,S(t) > B(t)\} = \{t\,:\,H(t)+k(t)>0\}$.
\end{proof}

Figure \ref{fig:skoro} illustrates the idea.  Bed elevations $B(t)=b(X(t),Y(t))$ (gray) are determined along the characteristic.  The unconstrained surface elevation $\sigma(t)$ (red, dashed) generally dips below $B(t)$.   In the case shown, at $t_1$ we enter an active set which ends at $t_2$ when $\sigma(t)$ reaches its minimum.  The non-decreasing correction $k(t)\ge 0$ (shaded) freezes at its maximum once $\sigma(t)$ increases.  The constrained surface elevation along the characteristic, namely $S(t)=\sigma(t)+k(t)$ (solid blue), satisfies $S\ge B$ everywhere, with $S=B$ on $[t_1,t_2]$ and $\dot S = E$ elsewhere.
