\begin{abstract}
This is a very preliminary note about whether we can solve the main complementarity problem for the surface elevation of a glacier, in the case where we replace the actual surface value of the ice-flow velocity by a fixed velocity field, via a Skorohod-type formula.
\end{abstract}

Consider snow which falls at some rate $a=a(t,x,y)$, over a map-plane domain $(x,y) \in \Omega \subset \RR^2$ and a time interval $[0,\infty)$.  More precisely, suppose $a$ gives the signed surface mass balance rate \cite{Cogleyetal2011} in ice-equivalent meters per second, so that $a>0$ is the accumulation rate when snow is falling and $a<0$ is the rate of ice removal (ablation) when melt is occurring.  Suppose that the land is at elevation $b(x,y)$, assumed time-independent; this is the land surface elevation if the land is not ice-covered, and it is the bedrock elevation underneath the glacier.

Let $s(t,x,y)$ be the surface elevation of an evolving glacier.  The surface kinematical equation (or condition \cite{GreveBlatter2009}) for the glacier applies at the surface $s$, but that surface can never go below the bed $b$, so part of the mathematical model for the evolution of $s$ is the following complementarity problem:\footnote{Complementarity problems are the strong forms corresponding to variational inequalities \cite{KinderlehrerStampacchia1980}.}
\begin{equation}
s - b \ge 0, \quad
\frac{\partial s}{\partial t} - \bu|_s \cdot \bn_s - a \ge 0, \quad
(s - b) \left(\frac{\partial s}{\partial t} - \bu|_s \cdot \bn_s - a\right) = 0  \label{eq:ncp}
\end{equation}
Here $\bn_s = \left<-\partial s/\partial x,-\partial s/\partial y,1\right>$ is an upward surface-normal vector.

In the above equations the motion of the upper surface of the glacier combines the surface mass balance $a$ with the surface-normal component of the ice flow velocity $\bu$, namely $\bu|_s \cdot \bn_s$.  In fact the ice flow velocity solves a Stokes-type problem for the ice below, so the full glacier evolution model couples \eqref{eq:ncp} with equations determining the 3D velocity and pressure fields.  See equations (1.1)--(1.6) in \cite{Bueler2025}; the above complementarity problem \eqref{eq:ncp} is system (1.3) in that reference.

However, a simplified model arises if the coupled-solution velocity is replaced by a pre-determined 3D velocity field $\bu(t,x,y) \in \RR^3$ which is defined for every $(x,y)\in\Omega$ and $t\in [0,\infty)$.  In this note we consider the model in which \emph{the velocity field $\bu$ is data}:
\begin{equation}
s - b \ge 0, \quad
\frac{\partial s}{\partial t} - \bu \cdot \bn_s - a \ge 0, \quad
(s - b) \left(\frac{\partial s}{\partial t} - \bu \cdot \bn_s - a\right) = 0  \label{eq:pilencpearly}
\end{equation}
In total, $\Omega,a(t,x,y),b(x,y),\bu(t,x,y)$ are data and $s(t,x,y)$ is the solution.

The interior condition of the above complementarity problem is a linear advection PDE.  Denote $\bu=\left<u,v,w\right>$ for the velocity in components, and now write partial derivatives with subscripts.  Let $c = w + a$.  The interior PDE, which applies at ice-covered locations, is
\begin{equation}
s_t + u s_x + v s_y = c \label{eq:pilepde}
\end{equation}
Here $u(t,x,y)$, $v(t,x,y)$, and $c(t,x,y)$ are scalar functions given as data.  With this same notation, complementarity problem \eqref{eq:pilencpearly} is written
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:pilencp}
\begin{align}
s - b &\ge 0 \\
s_t + u s_x + v s_y - c &\ge 0 \\
(s - b) \left(s_t + u s_x + v s_y - c\right) &= 0.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

The interior condition \eqref{eq:pilepde} can be solved by characteristics \cite[section 3.2]{Evans2010}, so we state that result before modifying it to account for the inequalities in the complementarity problem.

\begin{lemma}
Suppose $u, v, c \in C^1([0,T] \times \Omega)$ and let $s_0 \in C^1(\Omega)$ be initial data.
For each $(x_0,y_0) \in \Omega$, let $t \mapsto (X(t),Y(t)) = (X(t;x_0,y_0), Y(t;x_0,y_0))$ denote the
solution of the characteristic ODE system
\begin{equation}
\frac{dX}{dt} = u(t,X,Y), \quad \frac{dY}{dt} = v(t,X,Y), \quad
X(0) = x_0, \quad Y(0) = y_0. \label{eq:chars}
\end{equation}
Then the unique classical solution of \eqref{eq:pilepde} with $s(0,\cdot,\cdot) = s_0$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
s\!\left(t, X(t), Y(t)\right) = s_0(x_0,y_0)
  + \int_0^t c\!\left(\tau, X(\tau), Y(\tau)\right) d\tau. \label{eq:charsol}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Differentiate \eqref{eq:charsol} with respect to $t$ using the chain rule and
\eqref{eq:chars}, and then verify \eqref{eq:pilepde} is satisfied.
\end{proof}

In the situation where the inequalities of \eqref{eq:pilencp} were absent, formula \eqref{eq:charsol} could become an explicit formula for $s(t,x,y)$ if the characteristics can be inverted.  That is, we get a formula for $s(t,x,y)$ if $x=X(t;x_0,y_0)$ and $y=Y(t;x_0,y_0)$ can be solved for $x_0,y_0$ in terms of $t,x,y$, in which case we could rewrite \eqref{eq:charsol} as
\begin{equation}
s(t,x,y) = s_0(x_0,y_0)
  + \int_0^t c\!\left(\tau, X(\tau;x_0,y_0), Y(\tau;x_0,y_0)\right) d\tau \label{eq:charsolagain}
\end{equation}
where it is understood that $x_0=x_0(t,x,y)$ and $y_0=y_0(t,x,y)$.  In practice the domain $\Omega$ is bounded, thus the characteristic curves for the horizontal velocity field $\ip{u}{v}$ can either be followed back to time $t=0$ where they have location $(x_0,y_0)$, or they intersect with the boundary of $\Omega$ at a positive time, in which case boundary values for $s$ must supply the value at that ``stopping'' time.

Then $S(t) = \sigma(t) + \ell(t)$ is the unique solution along the characteristic
satisfying

S - b(X,Y) \ge 0, \qquad \dot{S} - c \ge 0, \qquad
\bigl(S - b(X,Y)\bigr)\,\dot{\ell} = 0,
\label{eq:odencp}
\end{equation}
with $\ell$ non-decreasing and $\ell(0) = 0$.  In other words,
\begin{equation}
s(t,X(t),Y(t)) = \sigma(t) + \ell(t)
\end{equation}
solves \eqref{eq:pilencp} along each
characteristic \eqref{eq:chars}.

\begin{proof}
Apply the Skorohod reflection formula \cite[Lemma 6.14]{KaratzasShreve1991}
to $h(t) = \sigma(t) - b(X(t),Y(t))$, the unconstrained ice thickness along the

\end{proof}
